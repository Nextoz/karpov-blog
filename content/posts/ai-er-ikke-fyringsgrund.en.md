# AI Is Not Grounds for Dismissal

A Chinese court has ruled that AI is not a valid reason to fire employees.

China. Communist China.

At the same time, 1,000 AI researchers are organizing in a union. The people building AI want protection from what they are building.

It is a little as if Oppenheimer had joined the office workers' union.

Meanwhile, some people in Denmark still dream of less regulation, because the market supposedly handles everything. The same kind of market optimism we see every time a critical IT system fails and people suddenly rediscover paper, whiteboards and manual workarounds.

The point is not that technology is bad.

The point is that AI does not remove responsibility.

## What This Means For Software Teams

AI changes work. There is no reason to pretend otherwise.

But it does not make responsibility obsolete.

In software teams, I see three practical questions:

- Which tasks become easier with AI?
- Which mistakes become easier to make faster?
- Who is still responsible when AI suggests something wrong?

That applies to code. It applies to case handling. It applies to support. And it applies to DevOps, where a wrong change is not only embarrassing but can affect operations, data and people.

AI can write suggestions for code, documentation, tests and runbooks. Fine.

But someone still has to understand the system, review the change, run tests, follow logs and take responsibility for rollback if things go wrong.

## My Practical Position

We should not protect every existing task from technology. That quickly becomes conservatism in disguise.

But we should protect people from bad implementation.

For Danish companies, that means:

- Use AI to make capable people stronger, not as an excuse for blind cuts.
- Train employees in concrete AI workflows, not only in "prompting".
- Measure quality, error rate and lead time before and after automation.
- Have clear rules for data, responsibility and human approval.
- Let teams help shape the tools they will actually work with.

A liberal AI policy should not mean the absence of rules. It should mean clear, limited and testable rules that make it easier to build and use technology responsibly.

That is the difference between freedom and passing the blame to a tool.
